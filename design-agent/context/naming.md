# Naming: What Is This Thing?

Before naming it, answer the prior question: what kind of thing is it?

---

## The three framings

### 1. A service
You (Jacques) do work for clients. The brand is personal. "Ramphal.design" is the right home. Scales only as far as you can personally reach. Good for the first 3 clients. Doesn't scale past that without a team or a product.

### 2. An agent
You've built a thing that does the work. The brand is the thing, not the person. Can be referred to, licensed, deployed. Scales because the thing runs across multiple clients simultaneously. Eventually separable from your personal brand.

### 3. A studio practice
A named practice that sits under or adjacent to ramphal.design. You are the principal. The brand signals the methodology, not just the person. "Ramphal.design" becomes the home for the writing and the work; the practice has its own name.

**The honest answer:** start as a service under ramphal.design, brand the product separately once it's proven. The product name becomes referrable — clients say "we use X" not "we hired this guy Jacques." That's when the brand starts doing work for you.

---

## What the name needs to do

- Work as a consulting practice name AND a product name
- Signal quality/judgment, not just speed or AI
- Not sound like every other AI startup launched this year
- Be memorable to a Head of Design or VP Engineering
- Hold up in five years when the AI hype cycle has passed
- Work as a domain name

---

## Name directions with reasoning

### Direction 1: The seam

**Seam** — Jacques already describes his positioning as "the seam between design and engineering." A seam holds two things together. It's where the structural work happens. It's invisible when done right. Breaks when stressed.

Works as: Seam Studio, Seam Design, just Seam.

Why it's strong: it's not AI-branded (durable), it directly mirrors the existing personal brand language, it implies craft and precision, it's a physical metaphor that non-designers understand.

Why it might not work: "seam" has textile connotations that might read domestic. seam.design, seamstudio.com — domain availability unknown.

---

### Direction 2: Proof

**Proof** — dual and triple meaning: the proofing process (quality review before print/ship), proof as evidence (your output proves the system works), and mathematical proof (rigorous validation). "In proof" is the stage between draft and final — exactly where this service operates.

Works as: Proof Studio, Proof Design, Proof.

Why it's strong: immediate intuitive meaning for designers (proofing is a known word), implies rigor and validation without sounding bureaucratic, works as both a studio and a product name.

Why it might not work: Proof is a common word and likely has domain conflicts. Proof the bourbon brand. Proof the app (for design feedback — check this).

---

### Direction 3: Warrant

**Warrant** — authorization, guarantee, justification. "This component is warranted" — it passed. Legal/institutional weight. Implies accountability, not just quality.

Works as: Warrant Studio, Warrant Design.

Why it's strong: unusual in design contexts (distinctive), implies the human accountability layer explicitly, "warranted output" has real meaning, not AI-bro branding.

Why it might not work: legal associations might read as corporate/defensive rather than craft-forward. Less warm.

---

### Direction 4: Assay

**Assay** — the process of testing a material to determine its composition or quality. Used in metallurgy, pharmaceuticals, chemistry. You assay gold to verify it's real. You assay output to verify it meets the standard.

Works as: Assay Studio, Assay Design, just Assay.

Why it's strong: highly specific and unusual, implies precision testing, the "assayer" is a trusted third party who validates quality — which is exactly the human oversight role, historically connotes craft and standards.

Why it might not work: obscure, requires explanation, might read as pretentious to some buyers. May be hard to spell/remember on first encounter.

---

### Direction 5: Quorum

**Quorum** — the minimum number of members required for a decision to be valid. Nothing ships without quorum. Implies human agreement as a structural requirement, not an afterthought.

Works as: Quorum Studio, Quorum Design.

Why it's strong: directly encodes the human-in-the-loop principle in the name, parliamentary/institutional gravitas, memorable.

Why it might not work: might sound like a meetings/collaboration tool. Quorum is also a known brand in other sectors.

---

### Direction 6: Canon / Codex / Covenant

**Canon** — an established rule or principle. "Design canon" has meaning. Implies a body of rules that governs output.

**Codex** — a manuscript, a book of rules, a system of law. "The design codex."

**Covenant** — a formal agreement, a promise with binding force. The token contract as covenant.

Why these are strong: all imply rules-as-law, which is the core of what the service does (encoding design system rules as agent behavior). Authoritative without being corporate.

Why they might not work: Codex has strong existing brand associations (Adobe Codex, Codex AI). Canon is a camera brand. Covenant may be too religious-adjacent for some buyers.

---

### Direction 7: Margin

**Margin** — the space where notes live, where precision lives, where the work gets reviewed. Also: profit margin, margin of error, marginal notes. A double meaning between the editorial (the margin is where you mark corrections) and the business (margin is the point of the whole thing).

Works as: Margin Studio, Margin Design.

Why it's strong: designers understand margins immediately, the editorial meaning (corrections in the margin) directly maps to the validation/review workflow, it's humble and specific without being precious.

Why it might not work: might not read as premium enough. "Margin" also has a finance connotation that could confuse.

---

### Direction 8: Index

**Index** — Jacques's site already calls itself a record/index. "An index, not a portfolio." There's something interesting about extending this concept to the practice — the index is the system of record. The agent maintains the index.

Works as: Index Studio, Index Design.

Why it's strong: directly extends the existing personal brand language, implies organization and structure and searchability, not AI-branded.

Why it might not work: generic. Index is used everywhere.

## What not to name it

- Anything with "AI" in the name — dates immediately
- Anything with "Design" + generic noun (Design Bridge, Design Link, Design Flow — all taken and all forgettable)
- Anything that sounds like a SaaS tool (Designly, Framely, Workly — low credibility for a high-trust consulting engagement)
- A personal name variation (Jacques Design, JR Studio — too small, doesn't scale to a product)
- Anything that requires explaining the pronunciation
